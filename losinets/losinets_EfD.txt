    We establish a formal mechanical analogy for Maxwell's equations using compact
vortex structures in a Kelvin¡Voigt viscoelastic medium. The shear modulus G =
2/(4r02) is set by the collective Tkachenko mechanism (Biot¡Savart back-reaction

vector potential; incompressibility (K/G  ) uniquely fixes the Coulomb gauge
 À A = 0. All four Maxwell equations follow with localised sources; in the far field
(  0) the viscous current vanishes and the exact vacuum equations are recovered.
Electric charge is identified with the circulation invariant Q = 0r0; the conversion
constant R = KV = 0/G is a directly measurable mechanical quantity. The model is
valid for   /(cL)  1 and reproduces Coulomb's law in the static limit.

1 Introduction

1.1 Motivation: reducing electrodynamics to mechanics

The question of whether electromagnetic phenomena admit a mechanical interpretation has
been central to theoretical physics since Faraday. Maxwell himself, in his 1861 paper "On
Physical Lines of Force" [7], modelled the magnetic field as a system of rotating vortex
cells in a mechanical medium, with idle wheels transmitting the electric force between them.
Helmholtz [5] had just demonstrated that vortex filaments in an ideal fluid obey far-reaching
conservation laws; the structural analogy between vortex tubes and magnetic field lines was
a central intuition for both Maxwell and Kelvin [9].

    Although the programme of mechanical ether models was eventually abandoned in favour
of the abstract field-theoretic formulation as codified by Heaviside [8] and its successors, the
search for hydrodynamic analogues of Maxwell's equations remains scientifically valuable for
several reasons:

    À Heuristic and pedagogical value. A concrete mechanical picture provides physi-
       cal intuition complementary to the abstract formalism. Well-constructed mechanical
       analogies guide the search for solutions and generalisations that may not be visible
       from the field-theoretic perspective alone.

    À Superfluid and quantum-fluid analogies. Quantised vortex dynamics in superfluid
       helium and Bose¡Einstein condensates (BEC) provides a laboratory for field-theoretic
       phenomena at accessible energies [27]. The circulation quantisation  = nh/m mirrors
       the discreteness of electric charge; the phonon spectrum has the linear (photon-like)
       dispersion  = csk at long wavelengths. Formalising this correspondence suggests
       concrete experiments and motivates new theoretical connections.

    À Hypothesis generation. A structural isomorphism between vortex mechanics and
       electrodynamics constrains models of the physical vacuum and suggests mechanisms
       that are difficult to access from standard field theory.

    This paper is purely formal: we establish a mathematical isomorphism between the
equations of a specific mechanical model and Maxwell's equations. No ontological claim is
made that electromagnetism is mechanical.

1.2 The central difficulty and our resolution

The principal difficulty in constructing a hydrodynamic analogue of Maxwell's equations is
the magnetic sector : Maxwell's equations admit electromagnetic waves propagating at finite
speed c, whereas the vorticity equation in a Newtonian fluid,

is purely diffusive. Previous constructions have addressed this difficulty in various ways:
some introduce a hyperbolic closure by hand without mechanical justification, while oth-
ers restrict to irrotational or steady-state limits where the general incompatibility is not
resolved [13, 16, 17].

4
    Our resolution is to replace the Newtonian constitutive model with the Kelvin¡Voigt
viscoelastic model, motivated by a concrete mechanical property of vortex rings: a com-
pact vortex core (Hill-type solution) maintains its shape via pressure balance. When de-

sponse. This endows the exterior vortex-ring medium with a finite shear modulus G, turning
the purely diffusive NS medium into a wave-supporting K¡V solid. The damped shear-wave
equation then follows as an exact consequence of the constitutive law, with no ad-hoc as-
sumption.

1.3 Previous work

Formal fluid¡EM analogies appear in [12, 13, 14, 16, 17, 10]. Moffatt [15] studied magnetostatic¡
Euler-flow analogies. Darrigol [11] and Bokulich [10] provide historical accounts of the
Helmholtz¡Maxwell analogy. The present work distinguishes itself by deriving the hyper-
bolic wave behaviour from the Kelvin¡Voigt constitutive law, which is itself justified by the
elastic stiffness of compact vortex cores.

7.1 The conversion constant R = KV

The constant R = /c2 = 0/G = KV is the Kelvin¡Voigt mechanical relaxation time of the

    À KV is the time for viscous stress to dissipate an elastic deformation of the medium.

9 Limitations and Scope

The model is valid under the regime conditions (14). It demonstrates a controlled hydrody-
namic analogy, not a proof that electromagnetism is mechanical. Key limitations:

    À Elastic modulus: pressure-jump approximation. The shape-mode result Gshape =
       272/(175r02) (Appendix F, eq. (3)) uses the background Bernoulli fields only (frozen-
       flow approximation), ignoring the kinetic energy of the perturbed stream function (1).
       The full first-principles calculation would add a positive correction, so (3) is a lower
       bound on Gshape.

    À Linearity. The exterior equations are linearised. Nonlinear effects (vortex reconnec-
       tion, turbulence) may destabilise the circulation invariant Q.

    À Charge discretisation. The quantisation of Q requires either quantised circulation
       (superfluid regime) or a separate microphysical mechanism.

    À Relativistic regime. The analogy does not address Lorentz covariance or relativistic
       corrections.

The model predicts testable phenomena in quantum fluids and classical vortex systems:

   1. Coulomb-type vortex interaction. Measure the force between two compact cores
       as a function of r12 and circulations 1, 2. The model predicts F  12/r122 with
       sign reversal under circulation inversion.

   2. Hyperbolic vs. diffusive transport. Impulsively excite a compact core and measure
       the arrival time of the perturbation at distance L. Navier¡Stokes diffusion gives t

11 Conclusion

We have shown that Maxwell's equations of electrodynamics follow formally from the me-
chanics of compact vortex structures in a Kelvin¡Voigt viscoelastic medium, under the as-
sumptions of weak viscosity, small exterior perturbations, and wave-dominated propagation
(  1).

    The conceptual chain is:

   1. A compact vortex core maintains its shape via pressure balance, generating an elastic
       restoring force with shear modulus G  2/r02.

   2. The exterior medium of dilute vortex rings behaves as a K¡V solid with wave speed
       c = G/ and relaxation time R = KV = 0/G.

   3. The K¡V constitutive law gives, exactly, the damped shear-wave equation for the vector
       potential (23).

   4. Incompressibility gives the Coulomb gauge condition (26) without additional assump-
       tions.

   5. Maxwell's four equations follow: (II) and (III) are potential identities; (IV) uses the
       K¡V wave closure; (I) uses the Coulomb gauge and the Poisson equation for the scalar
       potential.

   6. Electric charge is the circulation invariant Q = 0r0, conserved by Kelvin's theorem.
       The Coulomb law follows in the stationary limit.

    All mapped quantities have concrete mechanical interpretations. In particular, R = KV
is a directly measurable property of the medium, not a fitting parameter.

Value in current form. The framework provides: (i) a mechanically self-consistent formal
analogy useful for heuristics, pedagogy, and intuition-building; (ii) concrete experimental
predictions for vortex interactions in superfluid systems (BEC, liquid helium); (iii) a bridge
between vortex dynamics and electromagnetic field theory that may guide new hypotheses
about wave-supporting media.
